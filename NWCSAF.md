Cloud optical thickness used in this study have been produced with the Eumetsat Satellite Application Facility center on Support to Nowcasting and Very Short Range Forecasting (NWC-SAF) https://www.nwcsaf.org/ postprocessing tools. We use satellite images of cloud optical thickness produced with the NWC/GEO postprocessing tools version 2018 using measurements from the Spinning Enhanced Visible and Infrared Imager (SEVIRI) on-board METEOSAT MSG-3 geostationary satellite. COT calculated using the observed and simulated reflectances at 0.6µm and 1.6µm channels and NWP fields. Furthermore, a parallax correction has been applied to the data.

The imager has a native resolution of 3km at nadir, but as the resolution decreases away from the sub-satellite point it is much coarser above South Finland (≈ 8 km). The cloud optical thickness product is available at nominal 15 min interval and has been remapped to Cartesian coordinates from the native satellite projection at 4 km resolution. We use NWCSAF data between 1-7 of July 2021 for a sub-domain which covers a region over the southern Finland (see Fig. NN). The image resolution is 250 × 250 pixels and have a nominal pixel resolution of 4 km. In this example we have set the number of basis functions to N = 900 to avoid over-smoothing when the results are transformed back to the original dimensions.

## Data description for scientific use

The **NWC SAF Cloud Microphysics (CMIC) product** is part of the EUMETSAT Satellite Application Facility for Nowcasting.  Its purpose is to retrieve micro‑physical properties of clouds using visible and near‑infrared satellite radiances.  An iterative retrieval algorithm compares observed reflectances with pre‑calculated **ice and water look‑up tables**; the algorithm tests the convergence of cloud optical thickness (COT) and particle size and then selects the appropriate phase.  For water clouds, the vertically integrated liquid water content is computed analytically using auxiliary data such as sun/satellite angles, surface albedo and emissivity, temperature and water vapour path[^1][^2].

The product provides a number of variables on a pixel basis:

- **Cloud optical thickness (COT)** – a dimensionless measure of the integrated extinction through a cloud.  It is stored in NetCDF as `cpp_cot` and scaled by a factor (typically 0.01) and offset (0.0) to obtain physical values[^6][^7].  High COT values correspond to thick clouds and, together with the ice water path, can indicate heavy precipitation[^4].
- **Cloud phase** (liquid or ice) – derived from the selected look‑up table.  Extended phase categories (clear, fog, super‑cooled, mixed, opaque, cirrus, overlap) are also available.
- **Cloud effective radius**, **liquid water path** and **ice water path** – additional retrievals that depend on the optical thickness and droplet/ice size.
- **Quality flags and error estimates** – variables such as `cpp_status_flag`, `cpp_conditions`, `cpp_quality` and `cpp_dcot` indicate retrieval conditions, quality and estimated uncertainty; for instance, a flag bit signals whether bad optical conditions or snow/ice were detected during the retrieval[^6].

The CMIC product is generated from geostationary and polar meteorological satellites with high temporal resolution (e.g., every 5–15 minutes for geostationary platforms).  COT and effective radius are retrieved **only during daylight**, since they rely on reflected solar radiation; at night the product provides only cloud phase[^5].  The spatial resolution depends on the satellite sensor (typically around 3 km for geostationary imagery).  Data are distributed as NetCDF files containing latitude, longitude and the microphysical variables, and are available to registered users through NWC SAF.  These characteristics make the COT variable suitable for demonstrating spatio‑temporal interpolation methods, as the dense temporal sampling captures rapid cloud evolution while missing values at night or under thin clouds require careful handling.
