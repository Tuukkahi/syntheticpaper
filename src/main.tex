\begin{abstract}
    We introduce a learned low-dimensional semi-Lagrangian advection operator for reduced Kalman filtering and smoothing.
    A neural network predicts a low-rank parametrization of the advection, and the induced state-transition operator propagates the filter.
    A one-step approximation bound links the expected reduced-basis approximation error to the spectral tail of the model error covariance.
    Experiments on partially observed satellite time series demonstrate the reduced-basis convergence, scalability, uncertainty behavior, and reconstruction gains from the learned state model.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Spatio-temporal reconstruction problems arise in applications where a dynamic field must be inferred from noisy and incomplete or indirect observations.
Examples include remote sensing satellite data, where measurements are often spatially and temporally irregular and corrupted by noise.
In such settings, it is not sufficient to produce static point estimates by interpolation but the constructed fields should also be temporally consistent.
Moreover, reliable uncertainty quantification is essential for downstream applications.
Although the exact dynamical model is typically unknown, the evolution is can still be approximated by assuming evolution is dominated by advection.
While we often do not have the exact the underlying dynamical model, the dynamics are frequently well-approximated by transport models where evolve primarily through advection.

Kalman filtering provides a principled method to solve linear Gaussian inverse problems where a dynamical model is available.
However, for high-dimensional state and observation spaces such as satellite data, the classical Kalman filter is computationally infeasible.
Reduced basis methods, such as the one introduced in~\cite{solonen} address this by limiting the posterior updates to a fixed low-dimensional subspace.
This approach has been successfully applied in large-scale problems dynamic tomography and general imaging problems.~\cite{xraykfdr,opticalflowkfdr}

Classical transport-dominated dynamic modelling relies on optical flow or variational formulations to estimate motion between consecutive frame~\cite{horn,lukaskanade,tvl1}, while more recently neural networks have been employed to learn transport dynamics from data.~\cite{flownet,pwcnet,raft}
These approaches are primarily designed to estimate dense motion fields and therefore do not naturally extend to filtering problems where the dynamical model must propagate information across the domain.

We introduce a learned dynamical model tailored to Kalman filtering problems.
Instead of predicting dense motion fields, we construct a neural network that produces a low-dimensional parametrization of the advection operator.
The motion field is constructed to lie in a prescribed function space, thereby allowing explicit control over model complexity and improving robustness to noise and model discrepancy.

In addition to the algorithmic formulation, we derive a one-step expected approximation bound for the reduced filter.
This bound relates the expected error to the spectral tail of the state model error prior covariance and thus provides guidance for selecting the reduced basis.
We show that the theoretical bound matches an empirical convergence rate in a lower-dimensional setting with real-world data.
\section{Materials and methods}
\subsection{Problem formulation}
We consider the problem of recovering a time-evolving spatial field $u(s, t), s \in \mathbb{R}^2$ from partial and noisy observations.
The field is observed at discrete time instances $t_k$ on a spatial grid, and we denote by $x_k \in \mathbb{R}^d$ the vectorized state containing values of $u$ at the grid points at time $t_k$ and by $y_k \in \mathbb{R}^m$ the corresponding observations.
We assume a linear observation model
\begin{equation}
    y_k = H_k x_k + v_k, \quad v_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R_k),
\end{equation}
where $H_k$ extracts observed components of the state.

The state evolution is governed by a transport dominated dynamical model, which we approximate using an advection operator parametrized by a neural network that can be written as a linear state model
\begin{equation}
    x_{k} = M_{k} x_{k-1} + w_{k} \quad w_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, Q_k),
\end{equation}
where $M_k$ is constructed from the learned advection field.

The objective is to estimate the posterior distributions $\pi(x_k \mid y_{1:k})$ and $\pi(x_k \mid y_{1:T})$ efficiently in a high-dimensional setting.

\subsection{Advection model}
We model the deterministic part of the state evolution with the advection equation that is in continuous form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adv}
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + F \cdot \nabla u = 0.
\end{equation}
Here, quantity $u: \mathbb{R}^2 \times [0, \infty)  \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, is transported by the vector field $F: \mathbb{R}^2 \times [0, \infty) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$.


For each grid point $s_i$ at the arrival time $t_k$, the semi-Lagrangian update computes the value at $s_i$ by tracing it back to a departure point $\tilde{s}_i$ at the previous time step $t_{k-1}$.
The new value is then interpolated from the previous field at the departure point.
That is,
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{s}_i = s_i - F_k(s_i),
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
    u_k(s_i) = I_h [u_{k-1}](\tilde{s}_i),
\end{equation}
where $I_h$ is the spatial interpolation operator, and $F_k$ the discrete advection field describing transport over one time step interval expressed in grid coordinates.
This is the standard semi-Lagrangian form that follows the vector field backwards to departure points~\cite[Chap.~7]{durran}.
The semi-Lagrangian methods have seen wide adoption in atmospheric modelling because they are stable over large grid-scale displacements without the stability time-step restrictions imposed by explicit Eulerian schemes~\cite{diamantakis}.

For a fixed advection field $F_k$, the mapping $u_{k-1} \mapsto u_{k}$ is linear in $u_{k-1}$ and after vectorizing the field to a grid, we write
\begin{equation}
    x_k = M(F_k) x_{k-1},
\end{equation}
resulting in the state-transition operator $M_k := M(F_k)$ in the Kalman filter.

In the experiments we apply $M_k$ matrix-free using nearest-point interpolation to avoid unwanted smoothing and constant boundary value replication for out-of-domain departure points.

\subsection{Neural network model}\label{subsec:nnmodel}
The semi-Lagrangian update requires the advection field $F_k$ at each time step.
We learn the field using a short sequence of previous images.
Instead of predicting pixel-wise advection fields on the full grid, we predict a low-dimensional parametrization of $F_k$.

The choice is motivated by the filtering setting, large modelling errors, and high noise of the data.
Dense optical flow methods can be sensitive to non-advective behavior in the images.
However, the Kalman filter requires a stable state transition operator as opposed to a high-resolution optical flow method.
Therefore, we constrain the neural network's output to a finite-dimensional vector space.
This allows as to exactly control the complexity of the model by selecting a basis for the vector fields reflecting the expected solution structure.

In this work, we choose quadratic polynomial advection fields,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^2, \mathbb{R}^2) =
    \left\{
        \begin{bmatrix}
            a_1 + a_2x + a_3y + a_4xy + a_5x^2 + a_6y^2\\
            b_1 + b_2x + b_3y + b_4xy + b_5x^2 + b_6y^2
        \end{bmatrix} \,
        \middle| \,
        a_i, b_i \in \mathbb{R}
    \right\}.
\end{equation}

Rather than predicting directly the polynomial coefficients, we predict the values of $F_k$ at six interpolation nodes $z_i \in \mathbb{R}^2$, $i=1, \ldots, 6$.
The network predicts one scalar component of the advection field at the interpolation nodes.
To recover the two-dimensional advection, we evaluate the same scalar-component network on the original image sequence and on the same sequence rotated counterclockwise by $90^{\circ}$ in the spatial dimensions.
The scalar output from the rotated input is then used as the second component of the vector field.
In the implementation, the original and rotated sequences are stacked along the batch dimension, so both components are evaluated in a single forward pass.

For each input sequence, the network output is therefore the vector
\begin{equation}
    [f_k(z_1), \ldots, f_k(z_6)] \in \mathbb{R}^{6}.
\end{equation}
The scalar output from the rotated input is interpreted as the second component in the original coordinate system.
\begin{equation}
    [F_k(z_1), \ldots, F_k(z_6)] \in \mathbb{R}^{6 \times 2}.
\end{equation}

We choose the interpolation nodes such that the associated Vandermonde matrix
\begin{equation}
    V_2 = (p_j(z_i))_{i,j=1}^{6} \in \mathbb{R}^{6 \times 6},
\end{equation}
is well conditioned, where $p_j$ denote the scalar quadratic monomials.
Because of the low dimensionality of the problem, the placing of the interpolation nodes can be done geometrically by setting one node at the origin and five at equally spaced non-symmetric points on the unit circle.

Predicting the nodal values instead of the basis coefficients improves the numerical scaling of the target, because a small changes in the nodal values correspond to controlled and small changes in the resulting advection field.

The parametrization is independent of the grid resolution.
The images can be resized to the required input resolution, while the predicted polynomial field can be evaluated for the original grid.


For predicting the scalar components, we use a 28-layer ResNet neural network~\cite{resnet}.
For each input sequence, the network produces the six nodal values and the full two-component field is obtained by rotating the input sequence as described above.

\subsection{Synthetic training data generation scheme}
Because real-world satellite datasets do not have ground-truth advection fields, we train the neural network with synthetic training data.
By simulating data with a wide range of spatial structures, noise levels, and flow patterns, we aim at training a model that generalizes beyond a specific data source.
Each synthetic sample is generated on demand during training, eliminating need for large dataset storage and providing a virtually unlimited supply of training data.

Each synthetic sample consists of an image sequence and a target advection field used as the training target.
The advection field is quadratic, while the image sequence is generated with additional time-dependent perturbations and observation-like artifacts.
Thereby the network is trained to recover the dominant low-dimensional transport from noisy data possibly not generated exactly by quadratic advection.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item
        \textbf{Initial Field Sampling}

        Initially we sample a random $\mathbb{R}^2$ field with the Matérn covariance
        \begin{equation}
            \Sigma_{\nu}(u,v) = \frac{2^{1-\nu}}{\Gamma(\nu)} \left(\frac{\sqrt{2\nu}}{\ell} \lVert u - v \rVert\right)^{\nu} K_{\nu}\left(\frac{\sqrt{2\nu}}{\ell} \lVert u - v \rVert \right)
        \end{equation}
        from the Matérn-Whittle stochastic partial differential equation (SPDE)~\cite{whittle63, lindgren}
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:maternspde}
            (\kappa^2 - \Delta)^{\frac{\alpha}{2}} X(u) = W(u), \quad W \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I).
        \end{equation}
        Samples can be then drawn by evaluating the inverse Fourier transform
        \begin{equation}
            X(u) = \frac{1}{(2 \pi)^2} \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \frac{e^{-i u \cdot \eta} \hat{W}(\xi)}{\kappa^2 - \Vert \xi \Vert ^{\frac{\alpha}{2}}} \, d \xi
        \end{equation}
        for different realisations of white noise, which is fast to compute with FFT.

    \item
        \textbf{Vector Field Sampling}

        A vector field is generated by sampling an integer magnitude within a specified range for each component of vectors at interpolation nodes.
        This is followed by a random global sign for each component to ensure diversity in all directions.
        The quadratic polynomial vector field is then solved, as described in Section~\ref{subsec:nnmodel}
        These vector fields serves as the training objective for the model.

    \item
        \textbf{Numerical Advection and Noise Injection}

        The initial field is advected according to the generated vector field, creating a temporal sequence of images.
        At each step, small random integer perturbations are added to the vector fields to mimic dynamic variability of the vector fields and to show the network training data where advection is not strictly defined by quadratic polynomial vector fields.
        The target vector field in training remains the unperturbed quadratic one.

        In addition, at each step additive spatially correlated noise with sampled similarly as the initial field but with a random amplitude drawn between $[\sigma_{\text{min}}, \sigma_{\text{max}}]$.
        This exposes the neural network to a broad range of signal-to-noise ratios to enhances its robustness for real-world data.
\end{enumerate}
\subsection{Prior-based dimension reduction Kalman filter}
We seek optimal state estimates and uncertainty quantification for the advective time series with partial and noisy observations.
To correct the neural network-predicted advection with the incomplete observations, we employ the Kalman filter.

We begin with the general linear Gaussian state space model
\begin{align}
    x_{k} &= M_{k} x_{k-1} + W_{k}, \qquad W_{k} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, Q_{k})\\
    y_{k} &= H_{k}x_{k} + V_{k}, \qquad V_{k} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R_{k}),
\end{align}
where $M_{k} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is the state evolution operator, and $H_{k} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d}$ the observation forward operator.

In our application, $M_k$ is the semi-Lagrangian advection operator constructed from the vector field predicted by the neural network.
The observations are direct but partial, so $H_k$ extracts the observed pixels from the state vector.

The Kalman filter recursively computes the Gaussian densities
\begin{align}
    \pi(x_k \mid y_{1:k-1}) &\propto \exp\!\left( - \tfrac{1}{2} \| x_k - x_{k \mid k-1} \|_{\Sigma_{k \mid k-1}}^2 \right), \label{eq:filterprior} \\
    \pi(x_k \mid y_{1:k}) &\propto \exp\!\left( - \tfrac{1}{2} \left( \| x_k - x_{k \mid k-1} \|_{\Sigma_{k \mid k-1}}^2 + \| y_k - H_k x_k\|_{R_k}^{2}\right) \right) \label{eq:filterposterior}
\end{align}
through the prediction and update steps.
Here we use the precision-weighted Euclidean norm arising from Gaussian densities
\[
    \|z\|_{A}^2 := z^\top A^{-1} z.
\]
For the derivation of the formulas, refer, e.g.\ to ~\cite{kaipiosomersalo}.

Because the state dimension $d$ equals the number of image pixels, direct manipulation of the covariance matrices is computationally infeasible.
We therefore apply the prior-based dimension reduction approach of~\cite{solonen} where the prior-based dimension reduction to static inverse problem is extended for the non-stationary Kalman filter case.

\paragraph{Reduced state parametrization:}
We introduce a fixed projection matrix $P_r \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ with $r \ll d$ and parametrize the posterior state estimate as
\begin{equation}
    x_{k \mid k} = x_{k \mid k-1} + P_r \alpha_{k \mid k}, \label{eq:posteriorparametrization}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{k \mid k} \in \mathbb{R}^r$ are coordinates in the reduced subspace and denote its covariance by $\Psi_{k|k} := \operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_{k|k})$.
We use the conditioning notation
\begin{equation}
    x_{k \mid \ell} := \mathbb{E}[x_k \mid y_{1:\ell}],
    \qquad \Sigma_{k \mid \ell} := \operatorname{Cov}(x_k \mid y_{1:\ell}),
\end{equation}
where $\ell$ denotes the time index up to which observations are used for conditioning.
In particular, $x_{k|k-1}$ denotes the prediction based on observations up to time $k-1$, $x_{k|k}$ the filtered estimate, and $x_{k|T}$ the smoothed estimate.
The reduced coordinates $\alpha$ inherit the same notation.

Substituting the above parametrization into~\eqref{eq:filterposterior} yields the reduced posterior density
\begin{align}
    \pi(\alpha_{k \mid k} \mid y_{1:k}) &\propto \exp\!\left(- \tfrac{1}{2} \left( \| P_r \alpha_{k \mid k} \|_{\Sigma_{k \mid k-1}}^2 + \| y_k - H_k (x_{k \mid k-1} + P_r \alpha_{k \mid k}) \|_{R_k}^{2}\right) \right). \label{eq:reducedfilterposterior}
\end{align}
Equivalently, we approximate the full posterior by its restriction to the affine subspace $x_{k \mid k-1} + \mathcal{R}(P_r)$.

\paragraph{Prediction step:}
The prior state mean and covariance propagate using $M_k$ as
\begin{align}
    x_{k \mid k-1} &= M_{k} x_{k-1 \mid k-1}\\
    \Sigma_{k \mid k-1} &= (M_{k} P_r) \Psi_{k-1 \mid k-1} (M_{k} P_r)^{\top} + Q_{k},\label{eq:predcov}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Update step:}
The Gaussian~\eqref{eq:reducedfilterposterior} can be given by its mean and covariance as
\begin{align}\label{eq:updatestep}
    \alpha_{k \mid k} &= \Psi_{k \mid k} (H_k P_r)^{\top} R_{k}^{-1} (y_k - H_k x_{k \mid k-1})\\
    \Psi_{k \mid k} &= \left( (H_k P_r)^{\top} R_{k}^{-1} (H_k P_r) + P_{r}^{\top} \Sigma_{k \mid k-1}^{-1} P_r\right)^{-1} \label{eq:psiupdate}
\end{align}
To evaluate the term $P_{r}^{\top} \Sigma_{k \mid k-1}^{-1} P_r$ efficiently, we apply the Sherman-Morrison-Woodbury identity.
First, let $A_k$ satisfy $\Psi_{k-1 \mid k-1} = A_{k} A_{k}^{\top}$, by e.g., Cholesky factorization, and define
\begin{equation}
    B_k := M_k P_r A_k.
\end{equation}
Then from~\eqref{eq:predcov},
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma_{k \mid k-1}^{-1} = (B_k B_k^{\top} + Q_k)^{-1},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma_{k \mid k-1}^{-1} = Q_{k}^{-1}
    - Q_{k}^{-1} B_k
    (I + B_{k}^{\top} Q_{k}^{-1} B_k)^{-1}
    B_{k}^{\top} Q_{k}^{-1},
\end{equation}
so only an $r \times r$ dimensional system must be inverted.
In filtering we typically assume $R_k$ and $Q_k$ are either diagonal or their inverses are known a priori and hence we can now compute the entire forward recursion of the filter with inversions only in the reduced subspace.

In the code implementation, we return the reduced posterior precision in Cholesky form
\begin{equation}
    \Psi_{k \mid k}^{-1} = L_k L_{k}^{\top},
\end{equation}
due to numerical stability and the subsequent RTS smoother recursion.

\paragraph{Recovery of pointwise uncertainty.}
Although the full $d \times d$ dimensional covariance matrix of the state need not, and often can't, be formed explicitly, we can recover the pointwise posterior variances from the reduced forms.

The parametrization~\eqref{eq:posteriorparametrization} produces the projected reduced covariance in the state space
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma_{k \mid k}^{(r)} = P_r \Psi_{k \mid k} P_{r}^{\top},
\end{equation}
or the pointwise variances
\begin{equation}
    \operatorname{diag}(\Sigma_{k \mid k}^{(r)}) = \sum_{j=1}^{r} (P_r \Psi_{k \mid k}^{1/2})_{:, j}^{2},
\end{equation}
and equivalently through a triangular solve in the precision Cholesky factor case without explicitly inverting $\Psi_{k \mid k}$.
This allows efficient recovery of pointwise uncertainty fields in the original state dimension.

\paragraph{Choice of the reduced subspace.}
The reduced subspace projection $P_r$ controls both the accuracy and computational cost of the filter.
With $P_r= I_{d \times d}$, we would recover the standard full Kalman filter from the above equations.
Unlike in the static setting where $P_r$ would typically contain the $r$ leading singular vectors of the prior covariance, we typically want to avoid updating the reduced subspace at each step in order not to suppress directions important at a future time as discussed in~\cite{solonen}.

Previously, $P_r$ has been calculated e.g.\ by leading singular vectors of an a priori chosen covariance matrix~\cite{xraykfdr} and from spline bases~\cite{opticalflowkfdr}.
Given that we assume the model error process covariance $Q_k$ admits a Matérn structure, we use the analytical truncated eigenvectors of the Matérn-Whittle SPDE~\eqref{eq:maternspde}.
In addition to improve numerical conditioning of the algorithm, the eigenvectors are normalized.

\paragraph{Expected approximation error.}
Fix prediction state $x_{k \mid k-1} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and covariance $\Sigma_{k \mid k-1}  \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$.

Define the analysis increments $u_k := x_{k \mid k} - x_{k \mid k-1}$ and $u_k^{(r)} := x_{k \mid k}^{(r)} - x_{k \mid k-1}$, so that $u_k - u_{k}^{(r)} = x_{k \mid k} - x_{k \mid k}^{(r)}$.
Consider the functional
\begin{equation*}
    \Phi_k(u) = \frac{1}{2} \Vert y_k - H_k(x_{k \mid k-1} + u) \Vert_{R_{k}^{-1}}^{2} + \frac{1}{2} \Vert u \Vert_{\Sigma_{k \mid k-1}^{-1}}^{2}.
\end{equation*}
Then $u_k$ and $u_k^{(r)}$ are the minimizers of $\Phi_k(u)$ over $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $\mathcal{R}(P_r)$, respectively.
In quadratic form, with $\Sigma_{k \mid k}^{-1} = (H_{k}^{\top} R_{k}^{-1} H_k + \Sigma_{k \mid k-1}^{-1})$
\begin{equation*}
    \Phi_k(u)=\frac{1}{2} u^\top \Sigma_{k\mid k}^{-1} u - b_k^\top u+ c,
\end{equation*}
with $b_k = H_k^\top R_k^{-1}(y_k - H_k x_{k\mid k-1})$, the minimizer satisfies $\Sigma_{k\mid k}^{-1} u_k = b_k$.
Hence, for any $v$,
\begin{equation*}
    \Phi_k(v)-\Phi_k(u_k) = \frac{1}{2} (v-u_k)^\top \Sigma_{k\mid k}^{-1}(v-u_k),
\end{equation*}
which is minimized over $\mathcal{R}(P_r)$ at $u_{k}^{(r)}$, hence $u_{k}^{(r)}$ is the $\Sigma_{k\mid k}^{-1}$-orthogonal projection of $u_k$ onto $\mathcal{R}(P_r)$.
Therefore,
\begin{equation*}
    \Vert u_k - u_{k}^{(r)} \Vert_{\Sigma_{k \mid k}^{-1}}^{2} = \Vert x_{k \mid k} - x_{k \mid k}^{(r)} \Vert_{\Sigma_{k \mid k}^{-1}}^2 \leq \Vert u_k - \Pi_r u_k \Vert_{\Sigma_{k \mid k}^{-1}}^{2}
\end{equation*}
With $C_k := \kappa(\Sigma_{k \mid k}^{-1})$,
\begin{equation*}
    \Vert x_{k \mid k} - x_{k \mid k}^{(r)} \Vert_{2}^{2} \leq C_k \Vert u_k - \Pi_r u_k \Vert_{2}^{2}.
\end{equation*}
Taking expectation yields
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{E} \Vert x_{k \mid k} - x_{k \mid k}^{(r)} \Vert_{2}^{2} \leq C_k \operatorname{tr}((I - \Pi_r) \operatorname{Cov}(u_k)).
\end{equation*}
Using the identity
\begin{equation*}
    \operatorname{Cov}(u_k) = \Sigma_{k \mid k-1}-\Sigma_{k \mid k} \preceq \Sigma_{k \mid k-1},
\end{equation*}
we obtain
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{E} \Vert x_{k \mid k} - x_{k \mid k}^{(r)} \Vert_{2}^{2} \leq C_k \operatorname{tr}((I - \Pi_r) \Sigma_{k \mid k-1})
\end{equation*}

If $Q$ is positive definite, $\Sigma_{k \mid k-1} \preceq \beta_k Q$ with $\beta_k = \lambda_{\max}(Q^{-1/2} \Sigma_{k\mid k-1} Q^{-1/2})$.
If, moreover, $P_r$ spans the $r$ leading eigenvectors of $Q$,
\begin{equation*}
    \operatorname{tr}((I - \Pi_r) \Sigma_{k \mid k-1}) \leq \beta_k \operatorname{tr}((I - \Pi_r) Q) = \beta_k \sum_{j > r} q_j.
\end{equation*}
Hence,
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{E} \Vert x_{k \mid k} - x_{k \mid k}^{(r)} \Vert_{2}^{2} \leq C_k \beta_k \sum_{j > r} q_j.
\end{equation*}
Particularly, for two-dimensional Matérn covariance,
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbb{E} \Vert x_{k \mid k} - x_{k \mid k}^{(r)} \Vert_{2}^{2} \leq C_k \beta_k \sum_{j > r} q_j \lesssim C_k \beta_k r^{-\nu}.
\end{equation*}

The bound shows that the approximation error decays at the same rate as the spectral tail of $Q$, which for two-dimensional Matérn covariance is of order $r^{-\nu}$.
However, the constants $C_k = \kappa(\Sigma_{k \mid k}^{-1})$, and $\beta_k = \lambda_{\max}(Q^{-1/2} \Sigma_{k\mid k-1} Q^{-1/2})$ are not controlled a priori.
In the reduced filter, $\beta_k$ is governed by the spectrum of $Q^{-1/2} \Sigma_{k \mid k-1})^{\top} Q^{-1/2})$ and therefore, is dependent of how $M_k$ shuffles the spectrum of the covariance.

Therefore, the bound should be interpreted as a theoretical justification for convergence but is not expected to be sharp in practice.
\subsection{Dimension reduction RTS smoother}
The fixed-interval Rauch-Tung-Striebel (RTS) smoother gives the algorithm to compute the state densities conditioned by both past and future observations
\begin{equation}
    \pi(x_{k} \mid y_{1:T}).
\end{equation}
For the derivation of the standard backward recursion RTS equations, we refer to any standard textbook, e.g.~\cite{sarkka}.

We express the smoothed state in the same reduced parametrization
\begin{equation}
    x_{k \mid T} = x_{k \mid k} + P_r \alpha_{k \mid T}.
\end{equation}

Using the stored Cholesky factors $\Psi_{k}^{-1} = L_k L_{k}^{\top}$ and state evolution operators $M_k$ from the forward pass, we define the whitened reduced state transition
\begin{equation}
    C_{k} := M_k P_r L_{k}^{-\top}.
\end{equation}

The reduced smoother gain becomes
\begin{equation}
    K_{k}^{s} = L_{k}^{\top}C_{k}^{\top} Q_{k}^{-1} - L_{k}^{\top} C_{k}^{\top} Q_{k}^{-1} C_{k} (I + C_{k}^{\top} Q_{k}^{-1} C_{k})^{-1} C_{k}^{\top} Q_{k}^{-1} \, \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d},
\end{equation}
where we again need to do matrix inversion in $r \times r$ dimensions.

The backward recursion is then
\begin{align}
    \alpha_{k \mid T} &= K_{k}^{s}(x_{k+1 \mid T} - M_k x_{k \mid k})\\
    \Psi_{k \mid T} &= \Psi_{k \mid k} + (K_{k}^{s} P_r) (\Psi_{k+1 \mid T} - \Psi_{k+1 \mid k}) (K_{k}^{s} P_r)^{\top}.
\end{align}
Similarly to the forward recursion, the standard RTS smoother is recovered with $P_r=I_{d \times d}$

\begin{algorithm}
    \caption{Reduced Kalman filter with neural network advection and optional RTS smoothing}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \Require Initial states $x_{-2},x_{-1},x_0$, projection $P_r$, initial $\Psi_{0|0}$, noise covariances $Q_k,R_k$, observations $y_{1:T}$
        \State Initialize $x_{0|0}$ and factorize $\Psi_{0|0}^{-1} = L_0 L_0^\top$
        \For{$k=1$ to $T$}
        \State $M_k \gets NN(x_{k-3:k-1|k-1})$
        \State Predict $x_{k|k-1} \gets M_k x_{k-1|k-1}$
        \State Form $\Sigma_{k|k-1} = (M_k P_r)\Psi_{k-1|k-1}(M_k P_r)^\top + Q_k$
        \State Compute $\Psi_{k|k}$ and $\alpha_{k|k}$ using~\eqref{eq:psiupdate} with SMW for $\Sigma_{k|k-1}^{-1}$
        \State Update $x_{k|k} \gets x_{k|k-1} + P_r \alpha_{k|k}$
        \State Store $x_{k|k}$ and $L_k$ such that $\Psi_{k|k}^{-1}=L_k L_k^\top$
        \EndFor
        \If{RTS smoothing is used}
        \For{$k=T-1$ down to $0$}
        \State Compute $C_k \gets M_{k} P_r L_k^{-\top}$
        \State Compute smoother gain $K_k^s$ and update $(\alpha_{k|T},\Psi_{k|T})$
        \State $x_{k|T} \gets x_{k|k} + P_r \alpha_{k|T}$
        \EndFor
        \EndIf
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
\section{Numerical experiments}
The neural network is implemented in PyTorch and trained on CSC Mahti HPC using a single compute node with 4 NVIDIA A100 40GB GPUs.

The Kalman filter is implemented in PyTorch to take advantage of GPU computing which leads to orders of magnitude reduction in wall clock time of filtering.
Using a single NVIDIA A100 40GB GPU, computing the prediction and update step takes 0.6 seconds in single precision, and 0.3 seconds in double precision with 2048 basis vectors and $256 \times 256$ dimensional state, and observation vectors without accounting for the time taken for observation forward model, and state model.

We assess empirically the approximation error of the reduced filter on coarsened COT data.
The test data is from the same region as in Figure~\ref{fig:examplecot}, but spatially coarsened to $97 \times 105$ grid to make the full reference filter computationally feasible.

Figure~\ref{fig:basisconvergence} shows the mean squared error between the reduced filter and full filter posterior means as a function of the reduced dimension $r$ in log-log scale averaged over 10 days of data with 26 assimilation steps.
The autoregressive model $M_k=I$ was used to ensure a consistent setup across different basis sizes.

The observed slope of $\approx -1.06$ is consistent with the one-step theoretical rate for a two-dimensional Matérn covariance with $\nu=1$.
Although the estimate is derived for a single analysis step under a common prior, the same observed rate averaged over time suggests that the dominant contribution of the approximation error is mainly governed by the covariance truncation.

Figure~\ref{fig:models} compares our neural network produced model operator to two standard optical flow methods.
The dual TV-L1 optical flow~\cite{tvl1} represents a classical dense variational method with total-variation regularization and an L1 data term, using the OpenCV implementation~\cite{opencv}.
The RAFT~\cite{raft} method is a neural network approach for dense optical flow estimation based on all-pairs correlations and recurrent iterative refinement, using the pretrained Torchvision implementation~\cite{pytorch}.
Both of these alternative methods estimate vector fields from two consecutive images and therefore, we average the latest two vector fields for the comparison with out 3 latest images approach.
